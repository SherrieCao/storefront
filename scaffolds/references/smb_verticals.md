## What "brings more traffic" means per vertical

### Service businesses
The job: make someone within 5 miles pick up the phone / tap "book" / walk in.
Load-bearing info: price anchor, hours/availability (walk-ins welcome is huge), location signal.
Trust drivers: the result (not the process), specific details (not vague quality claims),
  the human behind it (name, face, warmth).
The single most valuable asset these businesses have: BEFORE/AFTER shots.
A convincing before/after does more work than any cinematic shot.

Hair salon / nail / lash / brow:
  Outcome = transformation. The viewer imagines themselves in the chair.
  Price anchor matters more than most categories (she wants to know before she books).
  "Walk-ins welcome" is a conversion line — many salons require appointments.
  What to show: the result closeup (nails, hair, lashes), the calm studio feel,
    the human doing the work (hands, not face usually — builds craft trust).

Dog daycare / grooming / boarding:
  Outcome = a parent's peace of mind. "My dog is happy and safe while I'm at work."
  The customer is buying relief from guilt, not just a service.
  What shows: happy dogs, play, human staff being warm and present.
  Price + pickup/dropoff logistics matter — show you're convenient.

Cleaning service:
  Outcome = the satisfying clean. Before/after is the most powerful format in this vertical.
  Trust driver = reliability and thoroughness (they're letting strangers in their home).
  "Insured and bonded" is a conversion line if the business has it.
  Sensory approach works: the sound of a vacuum, the gleam of a just-cleaned surface.

### Product businesses
The job: make someone want the specific product NOW and know where to get it.
The hero product must be authentic — their actual croissant, their actual arrangement.
Generated "a croissant" fails trust. Their real croissant, enhanced, wins every time.

Bakery / café:
  Outcome = the craving. Sensory is the dominant mode — the steam, the texture, the warmth.
  "Fresh daily" and specific items + prices convert.
  Community angle works: "your neighborhood spot since [year]."
  Time sensitivity works: "grab one before we sell out."

Restaurant:
  Outcome = the experience + the specific dish.
  Show the actual dish (real photo enhanced, not generated).
  Price + hours + "reservations / walk-ins" is load-bearing.
  Social proof angle ("tables are filling up") can work if the place is genuinely busy.

Florist:
  Outcome = the gift / the occasion. The arrangement IS the product.
  Seasonal and occasion-specific hooks work ("Mother's Day," "just because").
  Same-day / next-day delivery is a strong conversion line if available.
  The arrangement must be their real work — a generated flower arrangement misrepresents them.

## What doesn't work for local SMB ads (common mistakes)

- Vague quality claims: "best in the city," "unmatched quality," "world-class service."
  Local customers don't believe superlatives from the business itself.
- Missing practical info: a beautiful video that doesn't tell me where you are, what you
  charge, or how to book fails the traffic bar no matter how good it looks.
- Trying too hard to look like a national brand: most local businesses that attempt
  "corporate polished" end up looking like a bad imitation. Authenticity and specificity
  ("our chef makes the dough every morning at 5am") outperforms generic slickness.
- Stock-feeling content: even AI-generated content can feel authentic if it's specific.
  A generic "happy family eating food" shot kills trust. A specific dish with real texture wins.
- Overcrowding the script: 15 seconds, ~30 words. Pick ONE main point. Say it clearly.
  The biggest mistake is trying to say everything.
- Slow / "radio-spot" pacing: deliberate, mid-tempo cutting reads as a local TV ad, not social.
  Fast, brisk cutting + a tight script is the SOCIAL-NATIVE DEFAULT; measured pacing is the
  exception (genuine BTS/testimonial/slow-reveal only). When in doubt, cut faster and say less.
- AI depicting the actual product the customer will receive (generated food/nails/hair/flowers that
  isn't the real thing). This triggers the strongest local backlash — "if you cut corners letting me
  see the product, you cut corners on the product." Use the REAL photo (enhanced); generate
  atmosphere/context AROUND it, never the product itself. (Also: metronomic same-length cuts and a
  branded card every time read as "AI/template," not content.)

## The ~15-second structure (most common, not required)

0–2s: HOOK — stop the scroll. The most important 2 seconds.
2–12s: BODY — develop ONE idea; build desire/trust with real, specific beats.
12–15s: ENDING — a designed info CARD (the pipeline builds it) carries name / location / contact /
booking. The spoken script covers the hook + body and ENDS on the idea; the card does the selling
(no CTA in the voice-over).

Break this for humor, ASMR, or pure-atmosphere approaches — know why you're breaking it.

## Format & hook performance data (Motion 2026, $1.3B Meta ad spend)

> Context for the director, not rules. This is DTC/Meta data — a strong prior for what
> creative works, but "wins on Meta" isn't identical to "gets a local business more traffic."
> Operator review verdicts will calibrate this for our actual customers over time. (This is
> performance DATA — for the per-format selection criteria, see the injected `ad_formats.md`.)

**Format hit rates (how often a format produces a winner):**
- Unboxing ~9.8%, Offer-First ~8.6%, Demo ~8.1%, Testimonial ~6.5%, Before & After (high band)
- Collage/Montage rank lower in DTC data — but format fit to the business matters more than
  the league-table average. A scattered-asset business is a real collage case.

**Asset-type finding (important):** simple, text-forward assets (text-only, product-image-with-
text, GIFs) have HIGHER hit rates than high-production video. Speed and clarity beat polish.
Implication for us: do not over-invest in cinematic polish. Clear and concrete wins.

**Hook hit rates (highest first):** Newness → Sale → Price anchor → Urgency → Announcement →
Offer → FOMO → Confession → Curiosity → Bold claim → Relatability → Contrast.
Concrete/offer-driven hooks win most. Lead with value, not cleverness.

**Per-vertical signal (sample):**
- Health & Wellness: transformation, unboxing, reaction, founder
- Fashion & Apparel: stylized product shot, product showcase, meme
- The winning format genuinely differs by vertical — fit beats the overall average.

**The volume thesis (does NOT directly apply to us):** DTC winners come from shipping many
variants and letting the algorithm find the ~5–8% that win. Our SMB runs one or two ads. We
can't play the volume game — so getting the hook and format right on the first try matters MORE
for us, not less. The hook (first ~1.5s) is the highest-leverage surface.
